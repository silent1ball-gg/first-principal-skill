Prompt:
People make potions to keep fruits fresh.

A system drifts toward an undesired attractor (decay, error, corruption). The direct approach — constant active correction — is expensive and fragile. Bottleneck landscape engineering is the alternative: reshape the gradient at the narrowest point of the decay channel, and let the system's own dynamics keep it in the desired basin.

The intervention is *local* in space and time; its effect on the trajectory is *global* in phase space. It works because you are not fighting thermodynamics — you are redirecting kinetics.

## Derivation layers

```text
Layer 0 – Object:
  Fruit rots. People don't want it to rot. They make potions to stop the rot.

Layer 1 – Mechanism:
  Degradation (oxidation, microbial growth, enzymatic breakdown) is slowed or
  blocked by introducing a counter-agent — an antioxidant, an antimicrobial, a
  desiccant. The potion changes the local environment so the decay pathway is
  kinetically or thermodynamically disfavored.

Layer 2 – Method-pattern:
  Identify the dominant decay channel. Introduce a barrier (chemical, physical,
  or energetic) that raises the activation cost of that channel. The barrier
  doesn't need to be perfect — it only needs to make the decay timescale longer
  than the useful lifetime. This is the "kinetic trapping" pattern.

Layer 3 – Paradigm:
  A system drifts toward an undesired attractor. Instead of fighting the drift
  continuously, you install a *single intervention* that reshapes the local
  energy or information landscape, making the undesired state harder to reach.
  The intervention is local in space and time, but its effect is global in the
  phase space of the system: the trajectory never visits the decayed basin.

Layer 4 – Meta-paradigm (bottleneck landscape engineering):
    1. Map the decay channel (where does the system cross from "fresh" to "rotten"?)
    2. Find the bottleneck (the rate-limiting step along that channel)
    3. Apply a local intervention at the bottleneck that reshapes the gradient
    4. Let the system's own dynamics do the rest
```

## The core move

The original difficulty: the system has a natural gradient toward disorder (entropy, decay). Fighting it directly is expensive and fragile.

The move that makes it easier: **change the shape of the space so the gradient no longer points toward decay.**

This requires:
1. **A model of the decay pathway** — intermediate states, activation barriers
2. **A minimally invasive modification** — change only what's necessary near the bottleneck
3. **A timescale separation** — the modified barrier only needs to hold longer than the relevant observation window

## Instances across domains

- **Food preservation (the origin image)**: antioxidants, antimicrobials, desiccants — each blocks a specific decay bottleneck rather than fighting all degradation at once
- **Catalysis in reverse**: a catalyst lowers a barrier to speed up a desired path; preservation *raises* a barrier to block an undesired path
- **Error-correcting codes**: you don't prevent all bit flips; you add redundancy so the most probable error paths don't lead to a wrong codeword
- **Immune memory**: you don't eliminate all pathogens; you pre-install recognition so the decay channel (infection) is intercepted early
- **Institutional checks and balances**: you don't eliminate corruption; you shape the incentive landscape so the path to corruption is longer and costlier than the path to accountability

## Distilled paradigm

> **Preservation is not resistance — it's redirecting kinetics at the narrowest point of the decay channel, so the system's own dynamics keep it in the desired basin.**

The potion doesn't hold the fruit fresh. It reshapes a single bottleneck so the *fruit's own trajectory* never visits the rotten state.
